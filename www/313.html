SULLIVAN COUNTY EMS PROTOCOLS

313 – SEIZURES

Assessment

  • Seizure (onset, duration, type, post-seizure, level of consciousness)
  • Medical (diabetes, headaches, drugs, alcohol, seizure history)
  • Physical (seizure activity, level of consciousness, incontinence, head and mouth trauma, vital signs)
  • Trauma (head injury or hypoxia secondary to trauma)

EMR

  1. Oxygen and airway maintenance appropriate to patient’s condition
  2. Protect patient from injury during active seizures
  3. If patient is actively seizing, consider therapy if:
  4. Unstable ABCs exist, patient has been actively seizing for 5 or more minutes, or patient has an underlying disease or condition that may be worsened by prolonged seizure (trauma, COPD, pregnancy, severe hypertension)
  5. Spinal precautions if appropriate
  6. Narcan IN if suspected opiate overdose (use auto-injector or follow manufacturer’s instructions)

EMT

  1. If febrile, cool patient per hyperthermia protocol and monitor
  2. Glucose check

AEMT

  1. IV NS TKO or INT
  2. Titrate Dextrose 50% PRN slowly until glucose levels normalize, avoiding rapid swings
  3. If no IV available, BS <80 mg/dL, and airway protected, administer 1 tube instant glucose
  4. If narcotic overdose suspected, Narcan 0.4 mg IV/IO/IM/IN (peds: 0.1 mg/kg) titrated to adequate ventilation; may repeat to max of 2 mg

Paramedic

  1. Place on EKG monitor; treat dysrhythmias per protocol
  2. Adults – If actively seizing:
    1. Ativan 1–2 mg IV/IO, or
    2. Valium 2–5 mg SLOW IVP/IO, or
    3. Versed 2–5 mg IV/IO, or 10 mg IN
    4. Repeat dose if seizure continues
  3. Pediatrics – If actively seizing:
    1. Ativan 0.1 mg q 1 min to max 1 mg
    2. Valium 0.2 mg/kg or Versed 0.1 mg/kg IV/IO/IN
    3. Valium 0.5 mg/kg rectal
    4. Repeat once if seizure persists for 2 minutes

Notes

  • Specifically evaluate for: active bleeding, trauma, eye deviation, pupil equality, oral trauma, urinary/fecal incontinence, limb weakness or hypotonia
  • Goal of Narcan therapy is adequate ventilation. Chronic opioid users may require smaller doses to avoid agitation or behavioral effects
