SULLIVAN COUNTY EMS PROTOCOLS

714 – INTRANASAL MEDICATION

Paramedic

Rationale

  • Medication administration in certain patients can be difficult, such as those actively seizing or medically restrained, where IV access is nearly impossible and poses risk of needle-stick injury.
  • The Mucosal Atomizer Device (MAD) allows certain IV medications to be administered into the nose as a mist that is absorbed through the mucosa into the bloodstream.

Indications

  • Emergent need for medication administration when IV access is unobtainable or unsafe
  • Seizures/Behavioral control – Midazolam (Versed)
  • Suspected Narcotic Overdose – Naloxone (Narcan) (EMR/EMT/AEMT/EMT-P)
  • IN medications must be in small, high-concentration doses; max 1 mL per nostril per administration

Contraindications

  • Active nasal bleeding or discharge
  • Destruction of nasal mucosa

Technique

  1. Draw proper dosage
  2. Expel air from syringe
  3. Attach MAD device via LuerLock
  4. Briskly compress syringe plunger to atomize

Complications

  1. Slow plunger push won't atomize properly
  2. Fluid may leak from nares
  3. Less effective than IV (slower onset, incomplete absorption)
  4. Use of vasoconstrictors (e.g. Neosynephrine) delays and reduces effectiveness

IN Medication Administration

  1. Midazolam precautions:
    1. Risk of respiratory depression – have airway equipment ready
    2. If hypotensive, give 20 mL/kg normal saline bolus
    3. Use pediatric dosing tools: 0.1 mg/kg
  2. Naloxone (Narcan): (EMR/EMT/AEMT/EMT-P)
    • Adult:
      1. 0.4 mg every 5 min until RR improves or max 2 mg
      2. Split dose between nostrils
      3. Use prefilled devices as instructed
    • Pediatric:
      1. 0.1 mg/kg (max 0.4 mg) until RR improves or max 2 mg
      2. Split dose between nostrils
